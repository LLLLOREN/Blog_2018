Foie Gras

Foie Gras is a traditional French dish known as "Foie Gras" in French, which is literally translated as "Foie Gras" in Chinese. "Foie" means liver, "Gras" means fat. This famous dish pushes the romance of French food to the extreme, and some people say that foie gras is the most suitable western dish for women. French foie gras is usually served lightly Fried with port or a dark sauce. Another recipe requires "special treatment," in which foie gras, a mixture of other ingredients, is boiled, cooled, sliced and served cold, or drizzled with sauces. The foie gras is usually treated with a blend of cognac, Armagnac, port and truffles.

The history of foie gras can be traced back more than 2,000 years to the Romans, who really discovered the delicious and fun of eating foie gras. In the beginning they ate figs with them, and presented them to Caesar, who considered them a good meat. Later spread to Alsace and the rural southwest of France, people gradually began to use foie gras to make meat jellies and pate, and eat with French bread, is both simple and approachable. It was not until the time of Louis xvi of France that foie gras was presented to the court for Louis xv. After tasting it, it was deeply loved by the king. Since then, foie gras became famous and was praised by many famous writers, musicians and artists at that time. In 1870, foie gras was first cooked. A pastry chef serves foie gras wrapped in dough and used at a reception. Since then, Strasbourgian has been associated with foie gras, and many people think it's the best foie gras in the world.
